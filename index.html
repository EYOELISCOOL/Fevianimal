Our Story

Compassion, one street and one shelter at a time.

FeviAnimals began with a simple observation: across Ethiopia's cities and rural communities, countless dogs live without shelter, medical care, or protection under the law. We exist to change that — not with pity, but with practical, sustained action.

We believe animal welfare and human wellbeing are deeply connected. A community that protects its animals is safer, healthier, and kinder for everyone in it — including its children.

Hundreds of Dogs Killed in Hossana After Rabies Outbreak

In July 2026, following the deaths of three children from rabies, community groups in the central Ethiopian town of Hossana forced residents to kill their own dogs — including many that had been vaccinated. Here are the facts.

Source: BBC News (2 July 2026)
"We estimate that 400 to 450 dogs were killed in just a few days."
Alaazar Kebede, local veterinarian

What happened

  • Community groups ordered residents to kill their dogs — vaccinated or not — under threat of arrest and a 50,000 birr fine
  • Some owners hanged or beat their dogs to death; others handed them over to be killed
  • Mayor Samuel Shigute called the killings "illegal" and said they were not ordered by his administration
  • Police stopped the mass killings within a day, but reports say pressure on dog owners continues
  • In Ethiopia, it is illegal to kill animals in public spaces or cause them to suffer in cruel ways

Responsible Pet Ownership

Owning a dog is a privilege and a responsibility. A well-cared-for dog is healthier, happier, and safer — for the animal and for the community.

Feeding

Provide fresh, clean water at all times. Feed your dog a balanced diet appropriate for their age, size, and health status. Avoid table scraps — many human foods are toxic to dogs.

Shelter

Dogs need a safe, dry, and clean place to sleep and take shelter from extreme weather. A doghouse or sheltered area with bedding is essential.

Exercise

Daily exercise keeps dogs physically and mentally healthy. Regular walks, playtime, and socialisation prevent boredom and destructive behaviour.

Healthcare

Regular veterinary check-ups, vaccinations, and parasite control are vital. Spay/neuter helps control stray populations and prevents certain cancers.

Grooming

Brush your dog regularly, check ears and eyes, trim nails, and bathe as needed. Regular grooming helps you spot health issues early.

Understanding Rabies & Prevention

Rabies is a viral disease that affects the nervous system of mammals, including humans. It is almost always fatal once symptoms appear — but it is entirely preventable.

How rabies spreads

The rabies virus is transmitted through the saliva of infected animals, usually via bites, scratches, or contact with mucous membranes. Dogs are the main carriers in Ethiopia.

Symptoms in dogs

Early signs include changes in behaviour, aggression, excessive drooling, difficulty swallowing, and paralysis. If you suspect rabies, contact a vet immediately.

Vaccination is the answer

Annual rabies vaccination is the single most effective way to protect your dog, your family, and your community. Vaccinated dogs cannot transmit the virus.

Community action

Support mass vaccination campaigns in your area. Educate neighbours about the importance of vaccinating their dogs. A vaccinated community is a safe community.

Ethiopian Animal Welfare Laws

Ethiopia has laws that protect animals from cruelty, but enforcement remains a challenge. Understanding the law is the first step toward stronger protection.

Current legal framework

The Ethiopian Penal Code criminalises cruelty to animals, including killing, torturing, and abandoning them. Offenders can face fines and imprisonment.

Gaps in protection

Despite these laws, many animals remain unprotected due to lack of enforcement, limited resources, and low public awareness. Stray animals are especially vulnerable.

What you can do

Report animal cruelty to local authorities. Advocate for stronger enforcement and better funding for animal welfare. Support organisations like FeviAnimals that work to protect animals on the ground.

Working together

We are working with policymakers to close the gaps in Ethiopia's animal welfare laws. Your voice matters — join us in calling for change.

Community Animal Welfare Programs

Animal welfare is not just about individual pets — it's about creating communities where all animals are treated with dignity and care.

Community engagement

When communities come together to protect animals, everyone benefits. Safer streets, healthier neighbourhoods, and a culture of compassion are the results.

Education and outreach

FeviAnimals works with schools, religious institutions, and local leaders to spread awareness about responsible pet ownership, rabies prevention, and animal welfare.

Stray population management

Humane solutions like spay/neuter programs, vaccination drives, and adoption events are more effective — and more ethical — than culling or mass killing.

How you can help

Start a community animal welfare group. Organise vaccination and adoption events. Educate your neighbours. Every small action adds up to big change.

How Children Can Help Animals

Children are natural animal lovers. Teaching them to care for animals builds empathy, responsibility, and a lifelong commitment to kindness.

Simple acts of kindness

Teach children to approach animals gently, offer fresh water to stray animals, and never tease or frighten them. Small acts of kindness make a big difference.

Learning about animals

Encourage children to learn about different animals, their needs, and how to care for them. Books, videos, and visits to animal shelters are great ways to learn.

School programs

FeviAnimals offers school programs that teach children about animal welfare, rabies prevention, and how to be responsible pet owners.

Becoming advocates

Children can speak up for animals — by reporting cruelty, raising awareness, and inspiring their families and friends to care. They are never too young to make a difference.

Emergency First Aid for Dogs

In an emergency, knowing what to do can save your dog's life. This guide covers the most common emergencies and how to respond.

Wounds and bleeding

Apply firm pressure with a clean cloth or bandage. If bleeding is severe, seek veterinary help immediately. Do not apply tourniquets unless absolutely necessary.

Poisoning

If your dog has eaten something toxic (chocolate, rat poison, certain plants), contact a vet immediately. Do not induce vomiting unless instructed by a professional.

Heatstroke

Signs include excessive panting, drooling, and weakness. Move your dog to a cool place, apply cool (not cold) water, and offer small amounts of water to drink. Seek veterinary care immediately.

CPR

If your dog is not breathing, lay them on their side, extend their head, and give rescue breaths (mouth-to-snout). Compress the chest at a rate of 100–120 compressions per minute. Continue until the dog breathes or you reach a vet.

Always call a vet

First aid is not a substitute for professional care. Always take your dog to a veterinarian as soon as possible after an emergency.
